Working-Class Themes in The Smiths’ Lyrics and Aesthetic

'I don't owe you anything' The Smiths and kitchen-sink cinema

In the chapter “‘I don't owe you anything’: The Smiths and kitchen-sink cinema” in the novel Why Pamper Life's Complexities?, author Cecília Mello examines the connections between The Smiths’ music and the kitchen-sick films, or British dramas of the 1950s and 1960s that focus on the struggles of working class life. These films are known for their humorous realism, rebellion, and poverty within a young individual's life (Mello 2016, 141). She provides an in depth look into the band’s choice of lyrics and band aesthetics, and how they were influenced by the working class realism during the time. 

Mello structures her argument by first presenting her claim, then supporting it with examples of aesthetics, plot elements, and direct quotations that reveal parallels between British films and songs by The Smiths. The band's aesthetic and lyrical themes closely reflect working-class experiences and serve as a form of advocacy for that community. The author draws connections from British films, A Taste of Honey and Saturday Night and Sunday Morning, noting their shared themes of social critique and emotional rawness—particularly evident in The Queen Is Dead album. She argues that The Smiths were more than just a pop band; they aspired to realism, rejecting fantasy in favor of everyday struggles portrayed with both humor and drama. Morrissey, the band’s lyricist, often quoted directly from these films, “borrowing the voice of these characters, bringing them back to life in his lyrics” (Mello 2016, 141). For example, “This Night Has Opened My Eyes” closely mirrors the plot of A Taste of Honey, which centers on an unwanted pregnancy (Mello 2016, 143). Morrissey drew from stories from the films, as well as used common themes of kitchen-sink cinema. In “Heaven Knows I’m Miserable Now,” the lyric “I was looking for a job and then I found a job, and heaven knows I'm miserable now” reflects themes of alienation and disillusionment, common in kitchen-sink dramas. Morrissey’s lyrics were commonly inspired from these films’ themes. 

Kitchen-sink films were a British film movement, showcasing the working class realities of life post-war Britain. Clarke refers to World War II as a "people's war," highlighting how it united citizens across class lines in a shared desire for victory. (Clarke 1996, 207).This post-war sense of collective identity and attention to everyday struggles continued to resonate in British culture, influencing not only film but music as well. (Chapman 1991). Mello examines The Smiths’ aesthetic and compares it to kitchen-sink films. She claims that their black-and-white vintage album covers and clothing—typically second-hand shirts or cardigans—indicate that they portrayed themselves as plain, simple, and ultimately as embodying a working-class persona. Kitchen sink films, like Saturday Night and Sunday Morning, center white working-class lives while omitting the racial diversity present in post-war Britain. Similarly, Warnes argues that The Smiths’ album covers exclude racial representation and overlook the band's indebtedness to Black Atlantic musical influences. (Warnes 2008, 140-147). This theme of kitchen-sink cinema even inspired the origin of the band name, The Smiths, as Morrissey says was, “the most ordinary name and I thought it was time the ordinary folk of the world showed their faces.” (Mello 2016, 152). Many of The Smiths' songs exaggerate misery to the point of absurdity, making their songs almost carefree or humorous, which is the intention of these films. (Mello 2016, 141). Through these comparisons, the author effectively demonstrates how The Smiths embody similar characteristics of kitchen sink realism, using their music to reflect the struggles and humor of working-class life. 

Mello presents her argument effectively and offers valuable insights into The Smiths, providing a well-structured exploration of the band's lyrics and aesthetics. She reinforces her argument by citing works such as A Critical History of the British Cinema by Roy Armes, which supports her claim that many British film directors were middle-class individuals portraying working-class life without critically examining their own privilege. She explains how this view is what dominates much of the kitchen-sink cycle. (Mello 2016, 138). Mello at times places too much emphasis on realism, overlooking the strong undercurrent of romantic themes that also define much of The Smiths’ music. The Smiths also express their political standpoint more poetically than overtly political; their writing is more personal and intimate. Mello could strengthen her writing by addressing counterarguments and acknowledging areas where information is uncertain. Recognizing different perspectives on the band's history would add nuance to her work and create a more balanced analysis. Through Mello’s analysis, it is prominent that The Smiths used the aesthetics and themes of kitchen-sink cinema to challenge pop music conventions and give voice to working-class alienation, thereby blurring the lines between popular culture and social critique.
